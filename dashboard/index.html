A Bayesian market maker for SPX 0DTE options

An inventory-aware market-making engine that prices ATM 0DTE SPX calls with Black-Scholes, infers informed order flow with a Kalman filter, and quotes bid/ask via the Avellaneda-Stoikov optimal-spread model — continuously delta-hedged against SPX futures across a 100-day backtest.

01

What the desk actually does

Price the option

Every tick, the ATM 0DTE call is repriced with closed-form Black-Scholes Greeks (black_scholes.py). Because time-to-expiry is measured in hours rather than months, gamma and theta are an order of magnitude larger than a textbook example — this is the whole reason 0DTE market making is a distinct, harder problem than making markets in monthly options.

Infer who's trading against you

The desk never sees whether an order comes from an informed trader or noise — only the aggregate order-flow imbalance OFI = (buy_vol − sell_vol) / (buy_vol + sell_vol). A scalar Kalman filter (signal.py) treats OFI as a noisy observation of a latent, mean-reverting "informed pressure" state and recovers its posterior mean in closed form — this is Bayesian updating without needing MCMC or a particle filter.

Quote around adverse selection and inventory risk

Bid/ask are set with the Avellaneda-Stoikov (2008) model: a reservation price that skews away from current inventory, plus an optimal half-spread derived from risk aversion (γ) and order-arrival decay (k) (market_maker.py). The Kalman posterior additionally skews the reservation price ahead of informed flow, so the desk asks more before a buy-driven price move, not after.

Hedge and mark to market

After every tick the book's net option delta is neutralized in SPX futures, with a small per-share transaction cost. Because gamma is only hedged continuously and not perfectly between ticks, occasional jump/gap-risk events still cost the desk money — which is where the realistic drawdowns in the P&L curve below come from.

04

Likely interview questions

Why Avellaneda-Stoikov instead of just quoting a fixed spread?
A fixed spread ignores two things that matter: how much inventory risk you're already carrying, and how much time is left before the option expires worthless or deep ITM. A-S derives the reservation price and optimal spread from a stochastic control problem — maximize expected terminal utility of wealth subject to inventory risk — so the skew and spread are principled functions of γ (risk aversion), σ, time-to-expiry, and order-arrival intensity, not hand-tuned rules.
Why a Kalman filter instead of a rolling average of order flow?
A rolling average treats every observation as equally reliable and has no principled way to separate signal from noise. The Kalman filter is the closed-form Bayesian posterior for a linear-Gaussian state-space model: it explicitly models how fast the latent informed-pressure state decays (mean reversion) versus how noisy each OFI observation is, and weights new information by the Kalman gain — which itself adapts as uncertainty shrinks or grows. It's the simplest model that's actually Bayesian rather than just smoothed.
What's the actual source of P&L here — is it just the bid-ask spread?
Spread capture on filled orders is the baseline edge, but it's not the whole story: P&L each tick is realized spread, plus the change in the delta-hedged book's mark-to-market value, minus hedging transaction costs. Since the book is only delta-hedged (not gamma-hedged), a large enough move between hedge ticks — the jump events in the simulation — creates real convexity risk. That's why max drawdown is a meaningful number here rather than a formality.
What would break this model in live markets?
Three things: (1) the informed-flow model assumes a single latent driver, but real order flow can be driven by multiple correlated signals (macro data, dealer gamma positioning, ETF rebalancing) that a scalar Kalman filter can't disentangle — a factor or particle filter would be the next step. (2) Fill probabilities here come from a stylized Poisson intensity function, not a real limit order book, so queue position and adverse fills near the touch aren't modeled. (3) 0DTE gamma is so large near the close that continuous re-hedging assumptions break down — you'd want to hedge on a gamma-triggered basis, not just every tick.
